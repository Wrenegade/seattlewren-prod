About fifteen years ago, a friend mentioned a man named Heinz Stucke — a German cyclist who, at the time, held the Guinness World Record for most miles traveled by bicycle. He'd been riding for decades. No real home, no real income, just a bike and a camera. I looked him up, read everything I could find, and filed him away in my mind as something between a legend and a ghost.

Last August, during my first European bicycle tour, I got to have a beer with him.

## Getting There

I've been bicycle touring for the better part of twenty years. Not epic cross-continent slogs — mostly one to two week stretches, done in chunks. I have a long-term goal to ride the entire West Coast, but lately I've been focused on Germany. I have family there, I left a bike there, and I'm learning the language. The alignment of reasons makes it easy to justify going back.

My 2025 tour started in Amsterdam and took me East and South through the Dutch and German countryside toward Kassel, where my in-laws live. The route ran through Münster, and somewhere between there and Kassel sat a small, quiet town called Hövelhof.

That's where Heinz lives now. After more than fifty years on the road — through over 190 countries — he came home. Not by choice, really. Age made that decision for him.

I'd seen on his Facebook page that he was bedridden, that he welcomed visitors, but that an overnight stay wasn't something he could offer. That was fine. I was staying in Paderborn anyway. So I planned a detour with nothing more than a rough address and a vague hope that knocking on a stranger's door wouldn't go terribly wrong.

## The Entrance

The address led me to what appeared to be a school complex. In the middle of it sat a squat white building that looked more like a storage shed than a residence. I rode slow circles around it. My GPS kept bringing me back. I got off the bike, walked the perimeter, and eventually peered through a window. There were _things_ inside — possessions, papers, the evidence of a life lived in a small space. I was in the right place.

I knocked on the obvious door and heard a voice from inside, shouting in German. I caught enough to understand: _call the number on the door._

Taped to the door was a small handwritten note:

_"Ich kann nicht mehr laufen."_

_I can no longer walk._

Below it, a phone number. I called it. Heinz picked up.

In heavily accented but clear English, he tried to direct me to the _real_ entrance — somewhere on the other side of the building. I walked around. Nothing. He kept saying "keep going," but there was nothing but overgrown brush and shrubs. I went back to the original door. He yelled at me. I went back to the shrubs. He yelled again. This went on for a good ten minutes — me walking back and forth, him hollering through the phone from somewhere inside, neither of us able to understand why I couldn't find a door that apparently existed.

Eventually I climbed over the brush, pushed through to a section of wall I hadn't reached yet, and found a window. A small mirror was angled toward me from inside.

_"Now I see you!"_

And then, almost anticlimactically, I found the door.

## The Room

He told me to come in, so I did.

The room couldn't have been more than fifteen by fifteen feet. There was barely space to stand. Cabinets covered every wall, stuffed with photographs, negatives, artifacts, and documentation from five decades on the road. A small table sat in the middle where he kept organizing and cataloging, putting order to a life most people would find impossible to comprehend. In the middle of it all was a bed, and on the bed was Heinz Stucke — small, older than I'd imagined, legs propped up, with sores that reminded me of my grandmother in her final years.

He welcomed me in like an old friend, shook my hand firmly, and immediately apologized: _"It's been a while since I've thought in English — forgive me."_ Then he started talking, and barely stopped for the next six hours.

He remembered everything. Specific roads in countries I'd never visited. The weight of his panniers on certain climbs. People he'd met once, forty years ago, whose names he could still recall. At one point I spotted the Guinness plaque on the wall — _Most Traveled Man_ — and there it was, physical proof of the thing I'd read about as a myth, hanging in a fifteen-by-fifteen room full of a lifetime.

## The Bike

After a few hours, I asked about the bike--the one I've seen in all the pictures of him.

He pointed me to the next room. And there it was — the bicycle. The one I'd seen in every article and documentary. Not behind glass, not on a pedestal. Just leaning against the wall like it was waiting for a grocery run. I picked it up. It was absurdly heavy — like holding a motorcycle. I turned it over in my hands, felt the re-welded spots in the frame, read the markings he'd written on it over the years. I'd felt something similar once standing in the Sistine Chapel. A weight of accumulated meaning that doesn't translate into words.

![](https://seattlewren.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/heinz/IMG_2126.jpeg)

In the same room was a folded Brompton. I asked if he had a preference. He paused, then laughed.

_"Brompton paid me."_

That was it. After fifty years on one bike, the choice came down to: someone offered him a salary to ride theirs. He liked that just fine.

## The Beer

He offered me water, beer, or food. I opted for a beer, more for the symbolism than the thirst. The kitchen held the smallest fridge I've ever seen — one of those cube-sized things you'd find in a dorm room. Nearly empty. A single sandwich sat on the shelf.

_"Don't eat the sandwich. That belongs to my helper. He'll be back tomorrow."_

I grabbed two of the last beers, brought one to him, and we cracked them open.

_Prost._

I was having a beer with Heinz Stücke. The man most people have never heard of and almost everyone should know.

He asked where I was headed on my little European trip.  I told him that I was headed to Iceland afterwards on my way back to Seattle.  Of course, he had been there a few times and told me about all the places I should see.  Then, within an arms reach, he grabs a photo off his wall and shows me:
![](https://seattlewren.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/heinz/IMG_2132a.jpeg)
"This is Gullfoss Falls" he says.  Even back then, they had a fence keeping tourist from doing exactly what Heinz did (see him?).  Having visiting there, it would have been hard enough to do it running on my feet.  Somehow, he dragged his bicycle down there AND had someone take the shot?  Incredible.

After a few hours I got hungry and rode to a nearby market, grabbing nuts, chips, grapes, chocolate, and a salad for myself. I brought some back for him.

_"I can't eat anything that will make me go to the bathroom."_

Right. So he just kept sipping his beer — warm now, he hadn't touched it — while I ate and listened.

## What I Thought About on the Way Back

After six hours, I had to go. I didn't want to. The feeling was almost identical to leaving my grandmother during her final years, when she'd been mostly bedridden but still sharp and full of life and wanting to share it with anyone who would sit still long enough. I hugged him as best I could, said goodbye, and rode the quiet stretch back to Paderborn in the fading light.

I kept thinking about stuff. Specifically, how much of it I have that I don't need.

I consider myself a fairly frugal, fairly minimalist person. But compared to Heinz, I'm a hoarder with anxiety about net worth. This man chose, deliberately and for decades, to live with essentially nothing. A bike. A camera. Camping gear. And even bedridden, stripped of mobility and most of the world's ideas about what constitutes a good life, he was _happy_. Not performing happiness — genuinely, transparently, full of it.

I've spent plenty of time daydreaming about winning the lottery or a startup paying off. On that ride back to Paderborn, those thoughts felt almost embarrassing. I have a wife I love, four kids who are healthy, a home I'm not leaving, and enough to be comfortable. I don't need more. I need to be present with what I have.

Heinz Stucke has lived in voluntary poverty for all his life and will die without regret. He relives his best days every morning from that little table, sorting through photos and negatives, completely absorbed in a life he chose and chose fully.

That's the thing. He _chose_ it. It wasn't hard for him because it was _his_.

I want to be happy like Heinz Stucke. That's a stranger goal to write down than it sounds. But after six hours in that room, I mean it completely.

_If you've never heard of Heinz Stucke, take an hour and look him up. The ride started in 1962 and didn't really end until his legs gave out. Some people spend their whole lives waiting to live. Heinz just went._
