## The Rabbit Hole

"How does China's government actually work?"

That's the question that started this.  Because everyone has an opinion about China and AI, but almost nobody can explain how their system actually functions.  Including me.  So I went looking.

### How China Is Set Up (Quick Version)

First, you need to understand that China doesn't work like the US.  At all.  There's no separation of powers the way we think of it.  The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) sits above everything — the legislature, the courts, the military, the regulatory agencies.  All of it.  The State Council (think of it like a super-cabinet) runs the day-to-day government, but the Party sets the direction.  When the Party says "build a national credit system," it's not a proposal.  It's not a bill that gets debated for three years.  It's a directive.  And the entire government apparatus moves.

This matters because when we talk about China's AI governance, we're not talking about something that went through a democratic process.  There was no floor vote.  No filibuster.  No lobbying.  No 523-46 roll call.  The Party decided.  The bureaucracy executed.  Done.

Think of it this way: In the US, if the President wants a new AI law, he needs Congress.  Congress needs committees.  Committees need hearings.  Hearings need witnesses.  Witnesses need to be scheduled around fundraising dinners.  That takes years.  In China, the equivalent of the President, Congress, and the Supreme Court all report to the same boss.  One meeting.  One directive.  Go.

That's how the Social Credit System happened.

### The Social Credit System

In 2014, the State Council published a plan to build a nationwide "social credit system" by 2020.  They missed that deadline — it's a massive project — but the infrastructure has been rolling out steadily ever since.

Here's the misconception everyone has: it's not a single score.  There's no app on your phone that says "Your Citizen Score: 742."  That's the Western media version.  The reality is messier — and in some ways, scarier.

It's a network of databases.  Think of it like if Experian, Equifax, TransUnion, the IRS, the DMV, the court system, your employer, your landlord, and Amazon all shared one backend.  That's the National Credit Information Sharing Platform (NCISP), managed by a department called the NDRC.  By early 2025, it had collected over 80.7 billion records covering 180 million businesses.

The system sorts everyone into two buckets: a "redlist" (you're good — think of it as a whitelist) or a "blacklist" (you're not).  There are three main blacklists: one for people who don't comply with court orders, one for industry-specific violations, and the travel ban list.

### What Happens When You're On The List

This is where it gets real:

- **Can't fly, can't ride.**  As of June 2019, 26.82 million air tickets and 5.96 million high-speed rail tickets had been denied.  That's 32 million travel denials.  Not for crimes.  For being on a list.
- **Can't buy.**  No property.  No vacation packages.  No private school for your kids.  No luxury goods on e-commerce sites.  No high-end hotels.
- **Can't work (in certain jobs).**  Blacklisted?  You're ineligible for government subsidies, civil service jobs, and management positions at state-owned companies.  In a country where the state owns a LOT of companies, that's a big chunk of the job market.
- **Can't borrow.**  Credit card applications denied.
- **Can't hide.**  Your mugshot goes up on LED screens on public buildings.  Your face plays before movies at the theater.  Your personal information gets published online and at public venues.  This is not an exaggeration.  This is policy.

### Real People This Happened To

**Lawyer Li Xiaolin** tried to buy a plane ticket in 2016 using his national ID.  Rejected.  He'd been blacklisted for not complying with a court order from 2015.  He was 1,200 miles from home.  Stranded.  He had to write two apology letters to get off the travel blacklist — but he stayed on the credit card blacklist.

**Journalist Liu Hu** was blacklisted after a defamation lawsuit — he'd been reporting on government corruption.  He wired the court-ordered payment to the wrong account by mistake.  Didn't matter.  Blacklisted.  Couldn't buy property, couldn't fly, couldn't take a train.

**Jia Yueting**, a business executive, was banned from buying anything classified as "luxury" — including plane and train tickets — for a full year because of a court dispute over his company's debts.  Not his personal debts.  His company's.

### Where It's Headed

The system keeps evolving.  In March 2025, the CCP's Central Committee released new guidelines to unify all the scattered local pilot programs into a more coherent national framework.  In April 2026, the NDRC formalized a "credit repair" process — basically, a way to petition your way off the blacklist if you fix the issue.  Think of it as the DMV for your social standing.

The current focus in 2026 has shifted more toward the corporate side — tracking businesses for trustworthiness and regulatory compliance.  The personal scoring system is still there, still growing, just less uniformly enforced than Western media suggests.  Different cities run different pilots with different criteria.  It's not one monolithic all-seeing eye.  It's more like...fifty slightly different all-seeing eyes that share notes.

And I need you to re-read something:

*"26.82 million air tickets and 5.96 million high-speed rail tickets had been denied."*

That's 32 million times someone tried to travel and was told "no."  Not because of a crime.  Not because of a warrant.  Because of a *score*.

*"Public disclosure of personal information, including display of photographs on LED screens in public spaces and in pre-screening content at movie theaters."*

Your face.  On a billboard.  Before the movie starts.  Because you didn't pay a fine fast enough.

And here's where it gets fun for me.

The left says "if we don't regulate AI, corporations will build their own version of this."  The right says "if we give the government any authority over AI, we end up exactly like this."

Both sides are scared of the same thing.  And they're both right.

## No, Seriously.  Let's Do It.

Fuck it.  I'm in.

China cracked the code.  They built a system that ensures compliance at scale.  No ambiguity.  No drawn-out lawsuits.  No 1,208 conflicting state bills (yeah, that's how many AI bills were introduced across all 50 US states in 2025 alone -- 1,208).  No patchwork.  No confusion.  One system.  One set of rules.  Everyone knows where they stand.

You know what we have?  Colorado says one thing.  California says another.  Illinois has its own deal.  Texas is doing whatever Texas does.  And the federal government?  They signed an executive order in December 2025 that basically said "stop making laws, states, we'll handle it."  Then handed it to Marsha Blackburn who came back with something called the "TRUMP AMERICA AI Act" -- and yes, that's the real name -- organized around protecting Children, Creators, Conservatives, and Communities.  The 4 Cs.  Very catchy.  Still a discussion draft.  Still not law.  Still not even close.

Meanwhile, China's system has been running for over a decade.

You want efficiency?  There it is.

## Let's Talk About What It Solves

Think about every problem we complain about in America with AI.  Seriously.  Every single one.

**Deepfakes?**  Solved.  In a social credit system, the platform that hosts it gets blacklisted.  The creator gets blacklisted.  No need for a 3-year congressional hearing about Section 230.  Just...handled.

**AI hiring discrimination?**  Solved.  Company uses a biased algorithm?  Blacklisted.  Can't get government contracts.  Can't get subsidies.  Executives can't fly.  Watch how fast they fix their models.

**Kids and AI?**  Oh, this one's easy.  Platform targets minors without consent?  Blacklisted.  Executives' faces on a screen at the local AMC before Moana 4 starts.  "This person endangered your children."  Problem solved by Tuesday.

**Copyright infringement for AI training?**  Blacklisted.  Company scraped your work without permission?  They're on the list.  Their CEO can't buy a vacation package.  Artists rejoice.

**AI-generated misinformation during elections?**  Blacklisted.  Platform that spread it?  Blacklisted.  Person who created it?  Face on a billboard.  Good luck running for school board now.

Every single issue we're spending billions of dollars in lobbying, litigation, and legislation to maybe-possibly-someday address?  China just...did it.  With a database and some rules.

Why are we overcomplicating this?

## The Beautiful Part

Here's what really gets me.  The beautiful part.  The part that should make every efficiency-loving, small-government, cut-the-red-tape American stand up and cheer.

There are no lawyers involved.

No discovery process.  No depositions.  No class actions.  No appeals that drag on for seven years.  No lobbying firms spending $400 million to water down a bill.  No think tanks publishing 200-page reports about why regulation is bad while simultaneously funding the campaigns of the people who write the regulations.

Just a list.  You're on it or you're not.  And if you're on it, your life gets harder until you fix the thing you did.  And once you fix it, there's a formal repair process -- they literally codified that in April 2026 -- and you can get off the list.

Compare that to the US where if you're wrongly denied credit, you get to spend 18 months fighting three credit bureaus who all have different data about you, none of whom are accountable to anyone, and all of whom profit from the confusion.

China looked at that and said "nah."

## So What Do We Actually Do?

I don't have a clean answer.  I'm not going to pretend I do.

The EU went one direction — regulate before the damage.  They banned social scoring outright.  First thing in their prohibition tier.  They looked at China and said "never."  And they built the most comprehensive AI law on the planet to make sure of it.  It's not perfect, but it's a line in the sand.

The US went another direction — or, more accurately, 50 different directions at once.  We've got states tripping over each other, a federal government that can't decide if it wants to regulate or deregulate, and an industry that's lobbying both sides while building the very tools everyone's arguing about.

And China?  They just kept building.

Here's what I actually think — and I've been chewing on this since the National Debt post.  The answer isn't in any one system.  It's in the tension between them.  We need the EU's instinct to protect rights before they're violated.  We need America's instinct to let people build things without asking permission first.  And we need China as the cautionary tale of what happens when the system has no off switch.

But here's the thing that keeps me up.  This stuff is being decided right now.  Not in 10 years.  Not "someday."  Right now.  The EU's high-risk AI rules go fully live in August 2026.  Blackburn's bill is circulating.  States are passing laws every month.  The architecture is being built while you're reading this.

And who's making these decisions?  Politicians.  Lobbyists.  Think tanks.  People who have a seat at the table.

You know who's not at the table?  You.

Good thing no one votes, right?  Good thing we're all too busy to call our reps.  Good thing we just assume someone else will figure it out.  Because that's been working great so far.

I'm not trying to be the voice of a generation.  I'm not qualified and I don't want the job.  But I can read.  And what I'm reading is this: the systems that will govern how AI touches your life — your job, your credit, your kids' education, your healthcare, what information you see and don't see — those systems are being designed right now.  By people who are not you.

And if you don't show up, they'll decide for you.  They always do.
