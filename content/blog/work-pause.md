Life’s unfair. You just got axed and you’re out of a job. You’ve got time on your hands — whether that’s a gift or a curse depends entirely on what you do with it.

I’m not going to sugarcoat this: the AI wave is real, it’s accelerating, and it’s going to sting in the short term. But I also believe it’ll be great for everyone in the long run. Really, really great. The problem is, you’re in the short run right now. So am I.

Full disclosure: I voluntarily walked away from a stable role of 7 years to get ahead of this wave. I planned for it and built runway for myself. I’m not trying to pretend I’m in the same boat as someone who suddenly found themselves out of a job with little to no runway. But I’ve been in similar situations before — twice, actually — and what I’m about to share comes from those experiences.

This article is not intended to give you any specific template for getting your next job. A lot of jobs we trained for are changing faster than anyone can predict. This is scary for anyone currently on a work pause—like me. The more I see these models automate, the less my 25+ years of experience is worth. I know I’ve got to change, now.

I’m hoping to create a community where we can share ideas for what’s working for us during our work pause.

## Set Adrift or Set Free?

A buddy of mine called me out of the blue recently and said he was “set free.” Upper 60s, single, no kids, frugal, made a decent living. I couldn’t be happier for him.

But for the rest of us? We’re adrift. We all have to go back to work eventually, but this little break might be exactly what you needed — even if it doesn’t feel like it yet.

Here’s the thing nobody tells you: work gave you structure that you didn’t even notice. Wake up, make coffee, get the kids ready, commute, meetings, come home, dinner, repeat. Lose that structure and the days start bleeding together. How you handle that will make the difference between a productive pause and one you’ll regret.

But, I get the need for one to stare at the walls for a while. For my first pause, I came out strong but then faded as the game went on. During my second pause, I intentionally set time to clear my head. My advice is take the time you need but watch out for making “staring at the walls” a routine. You can’t get that time back.

## Set a Deadline for Drifting

Everyone needs a different amount of time to decompress. I get it. But set a limit.

The longer you allow yourself to drift, the harder you’ll have to row later. I’m not saying you need to start grinding on Day 1. I’m saying pick a date — two weeks, a month, whatever your runway allows — and when that date hits, you start building.

For my first pause, I didn’t waste time as I jumped right into learning AWS. I was motivated, younger, had only one child, and an affordable mortgage. I figured “let me level up quick, then I’ll have fun” which is what I did. While that plan eventually worked, it took 3 months longer than I anticipated. And, those were some stressful months.

For my second pause, I needed to decompress after my mom’s death [(read about that here)](https://jonnywren.com/blog/mom-post/). At this time, I had a much bigger mortgage and 2 young kids. But, if you read my story, you’ll understand why I had to take a break from it all even if that was going to cost me big.

Everyone’s situation is different. You might keep going, you might need time. Take what you need, I had to. But cap it as you have to start moving forward again.

## The Routine Framework

Here’s the core idea, and it’s simpler than you think:

Keep your good routines. Drop the bad ones. Build new ones.

That’s it. That’s the whole framework. Everything else is just execution.

### Keep

You already have routines that work. The time you wake up. The act of stepping outside in the morning. Whatever planning method you used at work — sprint planning, checklists, daily standups. Hang on to those and adapt them for home. They’re familiar, they provide structure, and you need that right now.

The most important one to protect? Your sleep schedule. Start sleeping until 10am and taking three-hour afternoon naps, and getting back to a normal rhythm when you land a job will be brutal.

### Drop

Now’s the time to evaluate whether a routine was helping or hurting. If your end-of-day routine was McDonald’s drive-through and three beers, you’ve got a built-in excuse to drop it: “I can’t spend the money right now.” That’s not discipline — that’s just math. Use it.

The easiest habit I dropped was late day drinking. I had worked in a start up where they had a well-stocked stash of booze for the taking. About every day before ending work a lot of us would kick back a few. Dropping this habit helped in many different ways for my well being.

### Build

This is where it gets interesting, and where most people get stuck. If you find yourself with nothing to do, you don’t have a routine problem — you have an imagination problem. Look around. There is plenty to do.

But you’ll procrastinate. Because you have time. “It can wait.” “Not now.” “Just don’t feel like it.” Those excuses don’t work anymore. The only honest excuse is “I don’t have a routine for this yet.”

## Start Small. Then Stack.

“I’m going to hit the gym every day.” Are you? Really? I’ve been a gym rat for almost 40 years and I won’t commit to going every day. That’s not a routine — that’s a New Year’s resolution. And those have a shelf life.

A real routine is small, easy, and almost impossible to fail at. Counting calories is a routine. Reading a nutrition label takes seconds. It doesn’t depend on a gym membership, your age, the weather, or your mood. It’s durable.

Once that’s installed, your body will tell you when Routine #2 needs to kick in. Destroy a bag of chips? Your internal voice says “go burn that off.” You don’t need motivation. You need a trigger that’s already built into Routine #1.

The principle: the more independent a routine is — the fewer things it depends on — the more durable it is and the more you can stack on top of it. Start with routines that require nothing but you showing up.

I’ve written a separate post about my routines [here](https://jonnywren.com/blog/my-routines). It’s taken close to 10 years to make some of them routines so it’s important to be patient. Start with quick win routines and then move onto ones that require more time.

## AI Is Your New School

Like many of you, I started tinkering with ChatGPT when it came out. It amazed me, but I didn’t see the daily relevance at first. Now I spend a minimum of two hours a day with AI, and it’s changed everything.

But here’s the key: I don’t use it to find a job. I use it to learn.

It’s free college where you set the curriculum. It won’t teach you things you need to know — only things you want to learn. You still have to show up and do the work. My kids go to school every day. Why should I be any different?

Here are some things I’m using AI for:

1. Healthy Cooking Recipes: We have all kinds of different flours (almond, spelt, tapioca) and I don’t want to throw them away. But, I also want to eat healthy so I’ve been using AI to help craft up some new breads that I’ll actually eat (or, at least know how many calories are in each)
    
2. My website, SeattleWren.com: This site was entirely built with AI. I didn’t write a line of code.
    
3. Photo Deduplication: There are apps out there that I could download but I don’t like downloading some app that I don’t know the origin. So, I had AI build me a deduplication app, with a front end GUI and all. Again, didn’t write a line of code and it works perfectly (and fast!)
    
4. Home Inventory App: We have a few freezers with a bunch of stuff in there that we can never remember. And, we’re too lazy to keep track so I created an Home Inventory app that took a handwritten set of inventory notes and built me a full blown app that both my wife and I use every day.
    

If you’ve never really used AI beyond asking it the weather, here’s your starting point. Copy and paste this into any AI model:

```
My name is [Your first name]
I live in the [general area]
I’ve worked as a [your last position]
I’ve been out of work for [your timeframe]
My experience with AI has been [describe it — more detail is better]

I read a post where I’m supposed to ask you 10 questions a day. Where do you suggest I start?
```

Anyone can do that. I can’t tell you what it’ll return for you, but that’s your starting point.

After a few weeks, you’ll progress in the complexity of your Q&A. You’ll know you are learning when you start going down rabbit holes to find more knowledge. It may lead to a dead end but you’ve probably learned a few things along the way that you didn’t know before.

Before you know it, you’ll have the ability to create yourself a simple website on your local computer. You continuously improve over time until you get to the point where you say “Hey, this isn’t bad. How do I make this public?”…and it will show you. That’s how I created SeattleWren.com.

Imagine you’re a painter with lots of artwork in the basement. But, to get your work public, you rely on galleries or word of mouth. Build yourself a website! Make it as artistic as you envision and model it to your style. Hey, you’re gonna finally sell some shit!

## Starting Is the Hard Part

Make no mistake: AI is automating jobs right now. We’re seeing it in 2026 and big business isn’t even trying to hide it. “We’re focusing on automation” — that’s AI.

So start. Build the routine. “Ask AI 10 questions a day.” That’s it. You can commit to that.

And no, “What’s the weather today?” doesn’t count. Ask it something that makes you learn. Ask it something that solves a real problem you have. Before you know it, you’ll be having AI build you a learning plan, design a website, or automate the tedious stuff in your life.

Hey, guess what? You’re learning how to automate your life. Don’t tell me that won’t translate to the new world of AI.

And you’re no longer drifting towards “What should I do next?” but rowing towards “What can I do next?”. Everyone in the world has access to the smartest person in the room (AI) so take advantage of it. It’s how other people are getting ahead, why not you?

As for me, I feel optimistic but that’s because I have produced some tangible benefits already (my wife loves my inventory app!). I can see how AI will change the digital world but I can’t quite see how it will affect the trades yet. But this is what excites me and I’d love to go down those rabbit holes with the wood workers, welders, painters, and cooks. Most AI communities are for coders but there isn’t one for tradespeople. I hope to build that with you all.

As Bill Murray said to Chevy Chase in Caddyshack “Hey, if you ever wanna rap or just get weird with somebody”…regarding AI, I’ll be there.
