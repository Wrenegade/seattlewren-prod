# Part 1: How We Got Here

### Losing My Dad

  

In early 2009, I lost my dad to pancreatic cancer. He was diagnosed in October 2008 and gone by January 2nd — just three months. The only good thing about cancer is that it sometimes gives you time to get things in order. We barely had enough.

  

My mom was going to be a widow, and I knew there was risk in that. She was a full-time stay-at-home wife. She never had a job, never paid a bill, and was fully dependent on my dad for anything that wasn't cooking or keeping a house. She lived in our childhood home in the suburbs of Chicago — a house that needed maintenance she had no idea how to manage. I was in Virginia. My brother was in downtown Chicago with his own family and business. She was going to be mostly on her own without the life skills for independent living.

### Who Mom Was

  

My mom was born in Japan in 1935. How she got to America and met my dad, I never fully pieced together. He said she was a Japanese language teacher and they met in New York. When I was young, I bought it. As I got older, it didn't add up — she never held a job in my lifetime. I never really asked, maybe because I didn't want to know.

  

What I do know: she was a solid, reliable housewife and a damn good cook. Between both my parents, we ate well and it was all home-cooked. I've kept that trait with my own family, and it's honestly the best thing they ever gave me.

  

She drove my dad to the train every morning at 6am and picked him up at 6pm. During the day she played cello and made arts and crafts. Many of the stuffed animals she made for me as a kid, I still have — my own kids play with them now.

  

She always complained about being "held back" by my dad, but that was her way of saying she wasn't satisfied with her life. Once he was gone, she thought she could roam the earth at will. My dad left her a reasonable inheritance and a paid-off house. Managed correctly, it could have sustained her. But she thought the money would last forever, and if it didn't, she'd just get a job and earn it back — at 72, with zero work experience.

  

### The Money Burns

  

Between 2010 and 2012, my mom executed a series of real estate moves that I could only watch from a distance. She sold the family house during the housing crisis — too cheap — and bought a high-end condo of equal value in a risky new development. A year later, she sold that and moved to Maui, shipping all her belongings across the Pacific. A year after that, she remodeled the Maui kitchen, "got bored," and moved to Orange County, California.

  

She never consulted me or my brother before any of these moves. When I confronted her realtor, I got "she's an adult." Each transaction — selling at a loss, buying at the wrong time, moving costs, taxes from staying less than two years — bled roughly $100K. By the time she landed in SoCal, I estimated she'd burned through $200–300K just moving around.

  

When I flew out to check on her, she had a roommate and was renting — which concerned me about her finances but also relieved me because at least there was no mortgage. Anytime I asked about money, I got brushed off. I tried to balance letting her live her life against making sure she didn't destroy it. That's a miserable line to walk.

# Part 2: The Scam

### The Day We Found Out

  

Christmas 2012. I was living in Georgia and had somehow convinced everyone — mom, brother, his wife and daughter — to visit for the holidays. It was great. Everything I wanted in a family visit, snarky fights included. My first child, a dog, a house, a Christmas tree.

  

During that visit, my mom told us she had won the lottery and would be giving each of us a pile of money. My brother and I looked at each other. There was a moment of excitement — then I started digging. There was no winning ticket. The payout was "coming soon," amount TBD.

  

She was being scammed. We tried to talk sense into her. She wasn't having it. She won that damn lottery and didn't want to hear otherwise.

  

The red flags were waving like at a parade, but neither of us could predict the extent of it. This was the moment I wish I could go back to and fix. If I could, she might be alive today._

### Everything Collapses

  

Mom went back to SoCal. We warned her, she seemed to listen, and we didn't press it. Then: she kicked out her roommate. Then she fell behind on rent. Then I was on the phone with her landlord, who told me mom had promised to pay once her "lottery winnings" arrived.

  

The landlord tried to intervene in person. My mom nearly broke her arm throwing her out. Empathy dried up fast after that — I got a deadline to move her out and a threat to call police.

  

Days later, California Social Services called. They told me about conservatorship — the same legal arrangement Britney Spears' father had. It would make me her legal custodian. Full control: where she lived, how her money was spent. But I'd have to convince a court she was mentally incompetent. The social worker assured me she was "more than qualify."

  

I couldn't do it. I could afford the process, but I didn't have the heart to declare my mother incompetent. _This was the second moment I wish I could go back to. She might be alive today._

  

In that same two-week window, my second child was born. I was grinding at Amazon with no real paternity leave. Then my brother called: mom had flown to Chicago and was trying to move in with him, staying at a $500-a-night hotel on credit cards.

  

I put her on a plane to Seattle the next day.

### The Damage

  

I set her up at an Extended Stay near our house and started excavating her finances. I discovered multiple bank accounts and maxed-out credit cards. At one bank, the employee was strict on privacy. At another, she was more sympathetic. "Look over my shoulder," she said.

  

In that one account alone, I watched $130,000 in only two transactions leave for somewhere. I didn't need to check the other banks. Everything my dad left was gone.

### Rebuilding

  

My brain kicked into problem-solving mode. What's working? She still had Social Security at $1,500/month, plus a lifetime annuity she'd wisely purchased — $1,300/month. Just under $3,000 to work with.

  

In 2014, even in Seattle, I could make that work. I found a $1,000/month apartment in Lynnwood, seven miles from my house. She could walk to the Asian grocery store, walk to the mall, and the leftover cash covered food and basics. Thank God she was a good cook — the one area where she knew how to stretch a dollar.

  

I tracked down her belongings in a SoCal storage unit, hired movers, and hauled everything north. Got her set up with furniture, kitchen, everything. Finances stable, roof over her head, food in the fridge. It was working. She still talked about her "lottery winnings" but it went in one ear and out the other — she had nothing left to be scammed for.

  

Or so I thought.

### The Feds and Jamaica

  

Digging through her paperwork, I found a propaganda letter saying "You've Won The Lottery" with a picture of Donald Trump on it. A logbook detailing pickups and drop-offs with dozens of 876 area code phone numbers. A document claiming my mom was a Federal Agent.

  

Local police were empathetic but under-resourced. They pointed me to the US Postal Service scam hotline. I called expecting nothing. Instead, I got a callback from a real Federal Agent who wanted everything I had.

  

I met two armed agents at a Starbucks in downtown Seattle, across from the courthouse. I asked why we didn't meet in the building. "Because that's a State building, and we're armed." Ah, right.  They listened to everything. They gave me solid advice on Power of Attorney and managing my mom. They were honest: this would be ongoing, and retribution wasn't guaranteed.

  

Sleuthing around on the Jamaican government website, I connected with a member of Jamaican Parliament who had publicly committed to stopping lottery scammers. He brokered a call with the Chief of Police for all of Jamaica. Weeks later, the detective on my case called: they arrested the guy. My Fed contacts confirmed it. I wrote an affidavit for trial.

  

But the Parliament member and detective had a warning I'll never forget: _"I'm sorry, but it won't end. Your mom's name is on a list. People kill each other for that list on a weekly basis. Someone else will call."_

  

They were right. Within months, a new scammer picked up exactly where the last one left off. Then a third appeared — "John Spencer" — who told my mom the second guy had been killed and he was now in charge of her winnings. Same playbook, word for word.

### The Hollowed-Out Book

  

In late 2014, my mom somehow moved herself to Wenatchee, WA without telling me. I was exhausted — two kids under three, Amazon grinding me down, back spasms I'd never had before. Part of me thought: good riddance.

  

I left her alone for two years. She visited for Christmas 2015; we visited her Christmas 2016. The apartment was a mess — mice everywhere, the kitchen a disaster. I found a shoplifting citation. Hundreds of Japanese bento boxes she planned to start a catering business with. An unusual amount of hard liquor for a woman who barely drank. She was more frail than I'd ever seen her.

  

By late 2017, she was homeless again — living in Super 8 hotels with her stuff in storage. I brought her to Seattle and moved her into my house. And that's when I found the worst of it.

  

In her belongings: another shoplifting arrest with a missed court date. A cashier's check for $20,000 from someone in Pennsylvania. And a red book, about twice as thick as a Bible. When I opened it, my jaw dropped — a rectangular hole cut into the pages, meant to be stuffed with cash and mailed. They couldn't squeeze money from her anymore, so they'd turned her into a money launderer.

  

I went straight to a lawyer for Power of Attorney. The scammers were calling every ten minutes, so I answered and told them I'd found everything. Then sketchy cars started parking outside my house. I installed security cameras and warned the neighbors. One was so excited he said, "Wow, I might actually get to use my gun!"

  

The last time a car appeared, I ran out and snapped a photo of the driver. As he pulled away, he said he'd come back and kill me. I stood there and told him to bring it.

  

The Feds traced him. A few weeks later, while a Federal Agent was at my house collecting evidence, a Jimmy John's delivery arrived. The receipt was signed "John Spencer." A message from the scammers — except the sandwich was vegetarian, wasn't even paid for, and I had to cover it. I told the scammers on the phone: thanks for the intel, the Feds are sitting right here.

  

That was the last direct contact.

### The Cashier's Check

  

I wasn't keeping the $20K. I tracked the FedEx sender — an elderly woman in Pennsylvania, clearly another victim. I found a possible relative on Facebook and LinkedIn, a young woman around 19. I cold-called.

  

Her mother called me back, terrified. I could hear the fear — she knew about the scamming. I assured her I was in the same boat and asked where to return the check. "Why don't you just cash it?" she said. Not a chance. I overnighted it back.

  

The next day, a relieved mother called to say she trusted me. We arranged a video call between my mom and the aunt. My mom quickly realized this woman wasn't going to pay any lottery winnings. She even said, "Oh, I'll never get my money" — proof she had some awareness it was a scam, but couldn't let go of the hope.

  

That's when I understood: no amount of evidence would fix this. It was an addiction that needed professional help I wasn't qualified to provide.

# Part 3: The End

### Stockton

  

Mom was determined to move near Yosemite. She'd never been to Stockton, California, but it was close on a map and affordable. For those who don't know, Stockton has one of the highest crime rates in the country — not ideal for an 82-year-old woman with no car. I flew down with her, vetoed the places she'd picked in the worst neighborhoods, and we found a complex with good security and walkable grocery stores.

  

We drove down together from Seattle in December 2017. It was nice — just me and my mom on a road trip with quality time and no drama. I have pictures from that drive. They're the last photos of my mom healthy and smiling.

  

I got her set up, grilled one last dinner outside her apartment, and flew home. I had full financial control. Rent, utilities, everything ran through me. Starting January 2018, I flew down every three weeks to check in.

  

Each visit, she looked thinner. More frail. She was complaining about her stomach, but I chalked it up to poor diet and tried to keep her stocked with healthy food.

### The Ulcer

  

In early May, I walked into her apartment and made her soup. Then I heard the sound of her vomiting. When I checked, the floor was covered in blood — including a fist-sized chunk of coagulated blood I'll never forget.

  

At Dameron Hospital in downtown Stockton, the diagnosis was a 6cm peptic ulcer — a 2.3-inch hole in her stomach. Basically, a knife wound.  The doctors wanted to know about her habits. She'd been popping six aspirin or ibuprofen a day for years. Combine that with her new love of alcohol plus years of unrelenting stress from the scammers, and her stomach had eaten itself alive.

  

I decided she was moving back to Seattle, end of discussion. She agreed. I flew home to get my car and drove back down. When I arrived, she was stable, alert, charming the hospital staff. They discharged her. I made a light meal — soup and salad, lukewarm, no dressing. She ate like a healthy person.

  

Then I heard something from the bathroom. She was sitting on the toilet, blank stare, gripping the edge of the sink. "Are you alright?" She shook her head no — and started falling sideways. I caught her, lowered her to the floor, tilted her head. She vomited again. The reddest blood I've ever seen. Maybe a quart.

  

I called 911.

  

This might sound horrible, but as she lay there, I had one fleeting thought: _should I even tilt her head sideways?_ That's how far gone I was. But I couldn't. I just couldn't.

### The Last Three Days

  

The doctors stabilized her and gave me two options: surgery with months of rehabilitation, or do nothing and hope it heals. I couldn't commit to months of post-surgical care. I asked about the odds of it healing on its own and they said better than 50/50 but she needs a special care facility which are always full.  But, for once, I caught a break:  They found her a spot at a care facility in Stockton — nearly impossible to get into, but they took her.

  

This was the best news I've heard in a long time.

  

For one evening, everything aligned. Mom was stable. She agreed to move to Seattle. We had a plan. I went back to her apartment with a bottle of Bacardi and started figuring out logistics. I was happy. Proud of myself, even.

  

At 2am, the phone rang. "You better get in here right away. I think she's going to go tonight."

  

Another episode. I rushed in. Nurses surrounded her. She was conscious, alert, but vomiting blood again. The doctor gave me two options: surgery or let her go.

  

I said it wasn't my decision — she was still conscious.

  

So I asked her: "What do you want, Mom?"

  

"Let me go."

  

"Ok."

  

They started morphine immediately.

  

I called my brother. Then I dug through her emails, found her niece in Honolulu, and set up an emergency Skype call with her sister in Japan. My mom, dying in a hospital in Stockton, got to see her sister's face one last time. Through tears on every end of that call, my mom said: "Don't feel sorry for me. I'm fine."

  

I think she was.

  

After the call, the morphine took over. She was unconscious but I kept talking to her. I'd managed my father and grandmother's deaths, and I'd learned that the moment you step away, they go. So I stayed. Three days. Bag after bag of morphine, each more potent than the last, locked in a glass box. The doctors couldn't believe she was still alive.

  

After three days, I said: "Mom, I'm going to go take a shower and be right back." But, just in case: "I love you" then a kiss on the forehead.

  

When I got out of the shower, my phone rang.

# To The Scammers

  

I know you'll eventually read this. If you can't read, have someone read it to you.

  

You killed her. Six years of false hope, fear, isolation, and lies — [you killed her](https://seattlewren.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/mom-post/namiewren.png).

  

I'm empathetic to a point. You might have families to feed. Jamaica doesn't have the same opportunities as the US. The dark side of me might even concede that Americans deserve a scam or two. But you took it far beyond what's fair.

  

You told her not to leave her apartment. You told her people were coming for her. Then you turned around and said you were her friend. You built trust by installing fear and never once came through. You had an 80-year-old widow stealing cell phones from Walmart. You paid for her moves to create distance from me. You never once showed your face.

  

I never asked for the money back. Take it, you fucking bastards. All I asked was that you be honest with her — just once. Tell her it was all a scam. She deserved closure. Instead, you kept her on a leash with a choke collar until it killed her.

  

Look at your children and tell them honestly what you do. But you'll lie to them too. They'd be ashamed if they knew the truth, and they wouldn't care that you're providing for them. Because they know this is wrong.

  

Karma is a bitch and it will catch up to you. 

  

I promise.
