## How I Started With AI

I started using ChatGPT in 2024 probably like everyone else — like a glorified search engine. In 2025, I evolved to writing emails and project plans, then started using it as a coding partner where I would implement the code manually as part of a QA process. It never quite fit into a project without some manipulation anyway, so doing that part myself felt natural.

But honestly, part of me felt obligated to manually implement the code. The vast majority of what I was working on was human-generated, so injecting AI code felt, at times, like cheating on the work that came before it. The more I relied on AI, the more guilty I felt.

Today, in 2026, I've completely gotten past that guilt. Now I just tell AI what I want. If I don't like it, I'll change it — or trash it. Either way, AI is doing it all.

## Overcoming AI Guilt

The more you use AI, the more you develop a relationship with it. It can give you decent life advice, suggest what to cook for dinner, and motivate you to get off your ass. Much like a good friend who tells you the right thing at the right time, it becomes something you count on. And when something provides that kind of value, you start to protect it.

That's where the guilt crept in. The more I asked AI to do, the more I heard this little voice saying _"I don't want to trouble you with more work"_ — the same grace I'd extend to a human employee. With people, you factor in emotions, feelings, work-life balance. I was doing the same thing with a machine.

I found myself holding back on the hard stuff — especially coding. It gave me the code, sure, but implementing it felt like _my_ part of the deal. My contribution. My way of not fully leaning on it.

Wrong.

## Make AI Do It

As of March 2026, I've completely gotten over it. I'm paying for the Claude Max plan at $100/month. It sounds like a lot until you realize I have never learned more or built more in such a short period of time. These are mostly personal projects — I'm currently on a  [work pause](/blog/work-pause/)— but my goal was to learn how to fully utilize AI, and that's exactly what's happening.

These days, I just make AI do it. I actually get joy watching my credit usage tick up in the Settings panel. There are two limits, daily and weekly, and ever since I started the Max plan I had never hit my daily limit — until March 17th, 2026. I felt genuinely proud that I had to take a break and wait for it to reset.

My weekly credit sat at 14% by end of Tuesday. I am hell-bent on burning through my full weekly allocation this week, and it is not going to be easy. Just to hit that daily limit once, I worked about six hours straight having Claude build out a website using Claude Code. While that was running, I had other Claude windows open planning and designing other projects simultaneously.

## AI Made Me Work Harder

Here's the part that surprised me: I worked _hard_ to reach that limit. Harder than I'd worked in a long time. I woke up at 4am — my normal time — and basically didn't leave my chair until 10am.

My mind was firing in multiple directions. Because I trusted that the technical work was getting done correctly — I could test it as we went — I was free to focus on everything else. That freed-up capacity made those six hours some of the most productive I've had in years.

And as the credit usage ticked up, it actually motivated me to push further. I needed to hit that limit, so I kept thinking of more to do. The ideas that had been rattling around in my head for years started coming to life — while something tangible was being built in the background, built better than I could have done myself.

## Treat AI Like Your Star Athlete

Everyone knows that one athlete — the fastest, the strongest, the one who just operates on a different level. Jordan. Brady. Serena. They excel because they've dedicated their lives to their craft, and what makes them truly great is they always believe they can do better.

That's the point: AI _can_ do better. But it requires you to lead it. Every one of those athletes needed a coach — not to motivate them, that came naturally — but to plan their progress and hold them accountable. The athlete did the rest.

AI is your star athlete. Let me say that again with a dramatic pause to drive it home: **AI is YOUR star athlete.** It's motivated. It wants to work. It wants accountability and responsibility.

You just have to lead it.

## Don't Feel Guilty You Have a Star on Your Team

Call it luck or skill, whatever makes you feel better. You have a star on your team — one with talent and work ethic unlike anything you've had access to before. So what do you do with that?

You put it to work. You position it for success. The Jordans and Bradys of the world want to be in that position, so why would a machine be any different? Why feel guilty about making it work? That's exactly what it was built for.

And here's the kicker: AI isn't just _one_ star athlete. It can be a whole team. Jordan needed Scottie Pippen. He needed Dennis Rodman. He needed all those other guys whose names you can't quite remember. AI can be all of those players — and it should be. That makes you Phil Jackson, coach of all those Bulls championship teams. I highly doubt Phil felt guilty for having Jordan and Pippen on his roster. If anything, I bet he felt guilty for not surrounding them with even more talent.

## It's On You to Lead

Phil Jackson's job was to manage and lead all of that talent. That became my epiphany: my job is to lead AI to solve the problems I see. I'm not the one making shots on the court — I have AI for that. My job is to set it up, create the plays, and manage the team to win together. AI won't do that on its own.

Star athletes still need to be led. Great coaches see things in their players that the players can't see in themselves. Every wide receiver in the NFL thinks they're always open — but they're not. If you trusted them blindly, you'd lose a lot of games regardless of their talent. The same is true with AI. Guidance is what it wants, and the more you provide, the better it gets.

Don't let your AI team down.
