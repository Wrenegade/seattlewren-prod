## Let's Learn From China!

Both sides of the aisle use China as the boogeyman.  The left says "if we don't regulate AI, corporations will build China's system."  The right says "if we give the government any authority over AI, we become China."

Both scared of the same thing.  Both right.

So you know what?  Let's just go there.  Let's learn from China.  Because they figured it out.  While we were busy introducing 1,208 AI bills across 50 states in 2025 — with 145 actually becoming law and none of them agreeing with each other — China built one system.  One set of rules.  Done.

Here's how it works.

They built the Social Credit System.  Started in 2014.  One national platform called the NCISP — think of it like Experian, the IRS, the DMV, the court system, your employer, and Amazon all sharing one backend.  80.7 billion records.  180 million businesses tracked.  You're either on the good list or the bad list.

Bad list?  Here's what happens:

Can't fly.  Can't take a train.  32 million travel denials and counting.  Can't buy property.  Can't send your kids to private school.  Can't get a credit card.  Can't work in government.  Can't manage a state-owned company.  And — this is the good part — your face goes up on LED screens on public buildings.  Your mugshot plays before movies at the theater.  "This person didn't pay a fine."  Roll the previews.

Deepfakes?  Platform gets blacklisted.  Creator gets blacklisted.  No 3-year congressional hearing.  Handled.

AI hiring discrimination?  Company gets blacklisted.  Executives can't fly.  Watch how fast they fix their algorithm.

Kids targeted by AI platforms?  Blacklisted.  CEO's face on a screen at the local AMC before Moana 4.  "This person endangered your children."  Problem solved by Tuesday.

Copyright infringement for AI training?  Blacklisted.  CEO can't buy a vacation package.  Artists rejoice.

Election misinformation?  Face on a billboard.  Good luck running for school board.

No lawyers.  No discovery.  No depositions.  No lobbying firms spending $400 million to water down a bill.  Just a list.  You're on it or you're not.

And if you fix the problem?  They literally built a formal process for that — Credit Repair Management Measures, codified April 2026.  You file a petition, they review it, you're off the list.  Compare that to fighting Equifax for 18 months because they have the wrong address for you.

Efficient.  Clean.  Solved.

So call your reps.  Tell them you want a comprehensive social credit system.  One national database.  Behavioral scoring.  Automatic enforcement.  And definitely the face-on-a-billboard thing.  Really underutilized deterrent.

I'll wait.

## Ok But How Did We Get Here?

Not "here" like 2026.  Here like...how did these two countries end up so fundamentally different that one builds a social credit system and the other can't agree on a single AI bill?

It goes back further than you think.  Way further.  And it's actually a pretty interesting story when you put them side by side.

### Two Revolutions

Both countries were born from a revolution.  That's the first parallel.  Neither one just...showed up.  Both were built by people who were pissed off enough to fight, and then had to figure out "ok, now what?" after they won.

But the *reason* they fought?  Completely different.

**America, 1770s.**

You're a farmer outside Boston.  You wake up before sunrise in a house with dirt floors.  Breakfast is porridge and beer.  Actual beer.  For breakfast.  Your kids are in the field by age 7.  You work sunrise to sunset, every day except Sunday when you're required to go to church.  You grow what you eat and sell what's left.  Life is hard but it's yours.

And then Britain starts showing up.

First it's taxes.  The Stamp Act in 1765 — a tax on every piece of paper.  Legal documents, newspapers, playing cards.  Not trade duties you can ignore.  A direct tax on your daily life.  And the part that really burned: you had no representatives in Parliament.  Nobody you voted for had a say.  "No taxation without representation" isn't a bumper sticker.  It's a structural problem.

Then it's troops.  1768.  Britain sends 4,000 soldiers to Boston.  Population: 16,000.  One in four people on the street is now a Redcoat.  And they're taking side jobs.  At lower wages.  So the government that's taxing you without your consent is also stationing soldiers in your town who are undercutting your pay.

1770 — the Boston Massacre.  Five colonists dead.  Sam Adams turns it into a viral moment with pamphlets and engravings.

1773 — the Boston Tea Party.  342 chests of tea in the harbor.  Organized civil disobedience.

1774 — Britain drops the hammer.  The "Intolerable Acts."  Shut down Boston's port.  Dissolved the Massachusetts colonial government.  And then — the Quartering Act.  British officers can now take over private homes to house soldiers.  Your house.  Your bed.  Without your consent.

That's the line.

Everything before that was a policy dispute.  Taxes, trade, representation — those are arguments.  But my house?  That's not a debate.  That's primal.

April 1775 — Lexington and Concord.  Shots fired.

July 4, 1776 — the Declaration of Independence.  27 specific grievances against King George III.  Most people remember "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."  The actual document is a list.  A legal brief.  Point by point:  you dissolved our legislatures, you taxed us without consent, you quartered soldiers in our homes, you cut off our trade, you waged war against us.

Eleven years from the Stamp Act to the Declaration.  A slow burn that ended with "this is my house.  Get out."

**China, 1930s-1949.**

Completely different starting point.  There's no "leave me alone."  There's "we're starving and everything is broken."

China in the early 1900s was in chaos.  The last dynasty collapsed in 1912.  Warlords carved up the country.  Famine.  Poverty.  Foreign powers — Britain, Japan, France, Germany — all had their hands in Chinese territory.  There was no functioning national government.  Just competing factions.

Mao Zedong was a 27-year-old nobody from Changsha who attended the first Chinese Communist Party congress in 1921 — 13 delegates in a house in Shanghai's French Concession.  Two reps from Moscow showed up and basically said "form a party."

By 1934, Mao's communist forces were surrounded.  Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists had 700,000 troops with cement blockhouses closing in.  So Mao did the only thing he could: he ran.  The Long March.  80,000 fighters broke out and marched 6,000 miles through mountains, swamps, and enemy territory over a year.  About 8,000 survived.  That march is their Valley Forge, their crossing the Delaware, and their origin myth rolled into one.

By 1949, Mao had won the civil war.  October 1, he stood in Tiananmen Square and declared the People's Republic of China.  Their July 4th.

Then he built the government.  And here's where it diverges from America completely.

Mao convened the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference (CPPCC) — a coalition that included non-communist parties, intellectuals, business leaders.  It looked like a "united front."  Everyone at the table.  That body declared the new republic.

Then Mao drafted a constitution.  Took a small team to a house near West Lake in Hangzhou in January 1954.  Their Independence Hall.  But here's the thing: he modeled it on the Soviet Union's 1936 constitution.  And the document created a new legislature — the National People's Congress (NPC) — where every member was vetted and approved by the Party.

The NPC approved the constitution unanimously.

Let me say that differently.  Mao wrote the document.  The document created the legislature.  The legislature approved the document.  And the CPPCC — the coalition with the non-communist voices?  It still exists today.  As an advisory body.  With no legislative power.

He used the coalition to get into power, then built a new institution that made the coalition irrelevant.

### The Parallel

Same problem to solve: "How do we govern after a revolution?"

| | **United States** | **China** |
|---|---|---|
| The room | Independence Hall, Philadelphia, 1787 | House near West Lake, Hangzhou, 1954 |
| The drafter | Madison, committee of delegates | Mao, 3 hand-picked writers |
| The process | 4 months of heated debate, 55 delegates | ~2 months, closed group |
| The vote | Ratified state by state, contentious, close calls | Adopted unanimously by Party-controlled legislature |
| The model | Montesquieu, Locke, Enlightenment philosophy | Soviet Union's 1936 constitution |
| Dissent | Built into the system | Not a feature |

Neither starting point is "wrong."  America's founders were fighting for "leave me alone."  China's founders were fighting for "someone take charge."  If your family is starving and warlords control your province and foreign armies occupy your ports, "someone take charge" isn't authoritarian.  It's survival.

But those two impulses — leave me alone vs. someone take charge — became the DNA of everything that followed.  And 75 and 250 years later, they're still running.

Huh.  OK.  They're not evil.  Just different.

## Not My House

Let me come back to that Quartering Act for a second.  Because I think people gloss over it and it's actually the whole thing.

The Quartering Act wasn't just "soldiers staying at your place."  It was the British government saying: "We have the authority to put someone in your private space, without your permission, and you will feed them and house them, and if you don't like it, that's too bad."

That broke people.  Not the taxes.  Not the tea.  *That.*

And it broke them because it violated something that predates politics.  It's not left or right.  It's not Federalist or Anti-Federalist.  It's "this is my space and you are not welcome in it unless I say so."

That instinct is literally why this country exists.  The Fourth Amendment — "the right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects" — is a direct response to what Britain did.  Jefferson and the founders didn't write that because it sounded nice.  They wrote it because they lived through the alternative.

This.  Is.  My.  House.

Now put the AI lens on that.

What's in your house right now?  Your phone.  Your laptop.  Your smart TV.  Your Ring doorbell.  Your Alexa.  Your thermostat.  Your kids' tablets.

And what's inside those devices?  Algorithms.  Deciding what you see.  Tracking what you buy.  Listening — maybe.  Scoring you for insurance rates.  Filtering your kids' content.  Recommending, nudging, profiling.

Did you invite that in?  Or did you click "I agree" on a Terms of Service document that was 47 pages long and written by lawyers specifically so you wouldn't read it?

The British at least wore a uniform so you knew who was in your house.

You think Jefferson — the guy who picked up a quill and wrote a formal "go fuck yourself" to the most powerful monarch on earth because soldiers were sleeping in people's homes — you think that guy would look at an algorithm that tracks your behavior, scores your creditworthiness, curates your information, monitors your purchases, and profiles your children...and say "yeah, that's fine.  I don't mind that one"?

He'd lose his goddamn mind.

And here's what's wild: China looked at the same technology and said "yes, that's exactly what we want.  Build more of it.  Connect all of it.  One system."  Because their founding instinct isn't "not my house."  It's "someone take charge."  And AI is the most powerful "take charge" tool ever invented.

Both responses make sense.  Given where each country started.

But we didn't start there.  We started with "get out of my house."

## So Where Are We?

Right.  2026.  Let's look at this thing now.

China's got their system.  Running for over a decade.  80 billion records.  It works — for their purposes.

The EU looked at China and said "never."  Banned social scoring outright.  First item in the prohibition tier of the AI Act — the most comprehensive AI law on the planet.  They also built a whole regulatory framework: risk tiers, conformity assessments, a dedicated AI Office with enforcement power, fines up to 7% of global revenue.  It's live.  Phased in since 2024.  Full rules hit August 2026.

The US?  We've got 1,208 state bills, a dozen conflicting state laws, one executive order that says "states, stop making laws," and a discussion draft from Senator Blackburn called the "TRUMP AMERICA AI Act" — organized around the "4 Cs": Children, Creators, Conservatives, Communities.  Not law.  Not even close.

Three systems.  Three philosophies.  One based on "someone take charge."  One based on "protect before it breaks."  One based on...well, we're still figuring that out.

Good thing no one votes.

## What Would Jefferson Say?

You know what, I think I know exactly how this conversation would go.  Let me play it out.

**King George** (who, for the purposes of this conversation, represents the "just monitor everyone and enforce compliance" school of thought):

*"Thomas, be reasonable.  The system works.  You track behavior.  You reward compliance.  You punish violations.  It's clean.  It's efficient.  No lawyers, no drawn-out trials, no messy democracy mucking up the process.  You want safe AI?  You want to protect children?  You want to stop fraud and misinformation?  One database.  One set of rules.  Everyone knows where they stand.  Your colonists would have been safer, more prosperous, more orderly.  You know I'm right."*

**Jefferson:**

*"George.  I wrote a letter about this.  You might remember it."*

**King George:**

*"The declaration was dramatic, Thomas.  But look at where you are now.  Two hundred and fifty years later, you can't pass a single law on artificial intelligence.  Fifty states doing fifty different things.  Your Congress can't agree on what day it is.  Meanwhile, the people you're supposedly protecting are being tracked by every device in their home — not by their government, mind you, but by private corporations who answer to no one.  At least in my system, there's accountability.  There's a list.  There's a process.  You get on the list, you know why, you fix it, you get off.  Your people can't even dispute an error on their credit report."*

**Jefferson:**

*"You done?"*

**King George:**

*"I'm making a point."*

**Jefferson:**

*"And it's a decent point.  I'll give you that.  Your system is efficient.  It solves problems.  Fast.  Clean.  No ambiguity.  I've read about it.  Impressive infrastructure.  80 billion records.  Really well-organized."*

**King George:**

*"Thank y—"*

**Jefferson:**

*"Who holds the list, George?"*

**King George:**

*"...The state."*

**Jefferson:**

*"And who watches the state?"*

**King George:**

*"The state has accountability mech—"*

**Jefferson:**

*"Who. Watches. The state.  Because I've met the state, George.  I've been the state.  And I can tell you from experience: the state doesn't watch itself.  That's not how power works.  Power doesn't self-regulate.  You know that.  You were the state.  You know exactly what you did with it."*

**King George:**

*"That's not—"*

**Jefferson:**

*"You put soldiers in people's homes.  That's what you did.  You had a system.  It was efficient.  It was orderly.  And you used it to put soldiers in my neighbors' kitchens.  Because you could."*

*"So yeah, your system solves problems.  But the problem it can't solve is itself.  And that, George, is why I wrote the letter.  Not because your system doesn't work.  But because it works too well.  In the wrong hands.  And all hands are the wrong hands eventually."*

*"Now.  I believe the phrase was...'go fuck yourself.  We're not doing it like that.'"*

## But I Was Serious

Go back and re-read the title.

*Let's All Learn From China How To Govern AI.*

I meant it.

Not "let's adopt China's system."  Not "let's build a social credit score."  Not "let's put faces on billboards."

Let's *learn* from it.

Because learning from something is not the same as becoming it.  And refusing to even look at how other systems solve problems you can't solve?  That's not independence.  That's arrogance.

You know what Jefferson actually did before he wrote the Declaration?  He studied.  Montesquieu.  Locke.  Roman law.  The Iroquois Confederacy.  He learned from everyone.  Then he built something different.  That's the model.  That's what the founders actually did.  They were students first.

China built a system that governs AI at scale.  It works — in a country whose founding instinct is "someone take charge."  There are things in it that are genuinely smart: unified databases instead of a patchwork, clear rules instead of ambiguity, a formal repair process, corporate accountability mechanisms.  There are also things in it that should terrify you: no independent oversight, no dissent mechanism, faces on billboards.

The EU built a different system.  Risk-based.  Rights-first.  Precautionary.  Banned the worst stuff outright, built graduated rules for everything else, stood up a real enforcement body.  It's not perfect — it's slow, it's bureaucratic, it might stifle innovation — but it's a line in the sand.

We haven't built anything yet.  We're still arguing about whether to argue about it.

So here's the actual ask.  Not the sarcastic one.  The real one.

Learn from China — what scale and speed look like, and what you lose when there's no off switch.

Learn from the EU — what it looks like to protect rights before they're violated, even when it's expensive and slow.

And then build something that's ours.  Something that starts with "this is my house" and figures out the rest from there.

Because people are thinking about this now.  China's been thinking about it for a decade.  The EU's been thinking about it since 2021.  Both of them have answers on the table.

You should too.
