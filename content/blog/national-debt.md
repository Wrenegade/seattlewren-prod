## Quick Review: What Is GDP, National Debt, Annual Deficit

- **Gross Domestic Product:** The total value of everything the country produces in a calendar year (Jan–Dec).    
- **National Debt:** The total amount borrowed (domestically and foreign) to cover what the Gov't spends fiscally (Oct–Sep).    
- **Annual Deficit:** The difference between how much the country spends versus brings in via tax revenue calculated off the GDP.

## Here's the analogy you care about

Joe Smith owns a small business called the Hoagie Hut.  His shop makes the best cheese-stakes in town and is a favorite among the local high schoolers.  From his business, after paying  employees and costs, Joe makes $100K per year.  He rents his house so he has no equity in his home.

Even though Joe makes $100K per year income (GDP), he spends $106K ($6K Deficit). Of that $106K,  $4K is just interest on past credit cards — so he's really spending $102K running the household.  The other $4K is the price of every year he didn't balance his budget (aka: deficit). Over time, he's racked up $122K in debt on his credit cards. No equity in it. He just has to pay it off.

Pretty hefty, but Joe's living a good life.

Two-thirds of that $122K is on his American credit cards and the remaining one third is on his international credit cards, mostly from Japan and China. And all of it is on adjustable rates. The introductory teaser period is ending soon and the new rates are going to hurt.  Joe knows it, but keeps kicking the can down the street.

Welcome to the U.S.!

## The Obvious Levers

It doesn't take a genius to figure this out. In order to pay that debt down, Joe's options are:

1. Grow, make more
2. Trim, spend less    
3. Do both    

**A Growth Example Could Look Like:**

1. Raise the price on his cheese-stakes and earn $106K to balance his budget. Debt holds steady at $122K — he's treading water.
2. Raise it even more and earn $107K. Now he's got $1K to start paying it off. Only 122 years to go.
3. That's 7% GDP growth needed just to start shrinking the debt, which is a big increase for his cheese-stake.  This assumes he won't lose any customers.  The US has averaged 2–3% GDP growth for the last 20 years. FYI.    

**A Trim Example Could Look Like:**

1. Reduce his spend to only $100K to balance his budget. Debt holds steady at $122K.
2. Cut to $99K. Now he can pay off $1K a year. Same 122 years.
3. But remember those adjustable rates? If the rate on his $122K climbs from 3.3% to 4.5%, his interest bill jumps from $4K to $5,500. His spending just went UP by $1,500 without buying a single thing. That surplus he worked so hard for? Gone.    

## Back to the Hoagie Hut
Joe's business has 5 employees.  He has less than 20 employees so he has no obligation to provide a health plan, but he does anyway.  Also, he has a wife and four children, ages 16, 12, 6, and 3.  None of them currently work.

First off, it's going to be hard to trim that $106K/year. Joe's got mouths to feed, rent to make, and debt to pay.

A Combo Example Could Look Like:
1. Layoff one of his employees and absorb the work himself (or put his oldest to work)
2. Still reduce his annual spend
3. Now his margin is higher but he's working a lot more and/or his oldest is missing out on his teenage years.

In any case, Joe's sacrificing something.  Whether that's his time and effort or a need/luxury, something has got to go.  And that layoff'd employee might have the same problem.

Another path to new revenue is to have his wife work a separate job from the business. Boom, new income and probably material — $50K per year. Now, the Smith's GDP is $150K a year! But, who's taking care of the kids? Yep, assume half of that new income goes to child care. So their spending just went from $106K to $131K. But their income is $150K. That's a $19K surplus. Things are looking up, at least from a financial perspective.

Suddenly, his 16 year old star linebacker starts eating more and his 12 year old just hit puberty, so he's devouring more food. Spending naturally went from $131K to $133K just because kids are growing. Surplus down to $17K. And, he's got 2 more behind them.

Then, a kid has a medical event. The $131K covers his health insurance but his deductible is $10K, and there goes that. Spending hits $143K. Surplus down to $7K.

Watch what just happened. Joe went from $19K ahead, to $17K, to $7K — and he hasn't even touched the $122K in debt yet. He's just been keeping up with new expenses.

Oh yeah, those adjustable rates. The $4K in interest he was already paying? Rates just ticked up. Now it's $5,500 — an extra $1,500 added to his spending that he didn't plan for. Surplus down to $5,500.

And his wife needs a car to get to work. She's been eyeing that Tesla. $500/month lease, insurance, charging — call it $9K a year. Joe went from a $5,500 surplus to a $3,500 deficit. Just like that, he's underwater again. But hey, at least the Tesla looks nice in the driveway.

After all that, Joe's got his wife working, a shiny new car payment, and he's worse off than when he started. Sad but true.

What's his best option now? Maybe his wife returns to stay at home to cut the childcare and the Tesla payment. Ok, but there goes the extra income and he's never paid down a dollar of debt. Put his 16 y/o to work making cheese-stakes and make him earn his keep? Not a bad idea but there goes high school football. Sell the Tesla? It depreciated 20% the moment they drove it off the lot.  

Oh, the easiest option…..borrow more.

Welcome to the U.S.!!
  
## The Number That Actually Matters

In order for the Smiths to pay down that debt, they would probably have to consider selling the Hoagie Hut.  Otherwise, at status quo, there is no chance. And, unless the US starts selling off States to other countries, neither with the US. End stop. The national debt has gone up every single decade since 1930. It's not getting paid off. Ever.

You can stop reading here if you want. But, before you bail, know this: — that's not actually the problem.

**What matters is the ratio of debt to income.** As long as Joe's income grows faster than his debt, he's fine.  He owes $122K but makes $100K. (1.22) If next year he makes $110K and owes $125K, he's actually in better shape (1.13) Because he's making more relative to what he owes, his debt-to-GDP ratio goes down.

After WWII, the US owed 120% of GDP. 1.20 ratio.  Sound familiar? The debt kept growing in dollar terms for the next 35 years. But GDP grew faster, and the ratio dropped to 31% by the mid 70s. Nobody "paid it off" and, in total dollars, increased every year. The economy just outgrew it.

Interesting, eh? We kicked some serious ass after WWII for about 30 years. So, what happened?

The debt-to-GDP ratio bottomed around 1974 at roughly 31%, then started climbing again through the 80s with defense spending and tax cuts. By 1984, the ratio was already heading back up.  As a country, we decided that it was important to have the most terrifying and intimidating National Defense the world has ever scene. That’s the first hump which continues into today. But, it’s not THAT much.

Then, you’ll see a little dip in the late 90s. For us Gen X’ers, remember the dot.com boom? That’s part of it thanks to the massive increase in tax revenue from capital gains. But, what else? Turns out, Congress and the Administration actually worked together and balanced the budget. Not only that, they created a small surplus to actually pay down a little. The surpluses ran during fiscal years starting in ‘98 and the good times kept rolling until ‘01, spanning two administrations.

Then the dot.com bust, 9/11, the war in Afghanistan, the war in Iraq, even more tax cuts, Medicare Part D, and finally the creation of Homeland Security.

*Back to the Smiths*
For about 25 years, the family was doing great. Then their stock portfolio took a hit (dot.com bust).  Someone broke into the Hoagie Hut and ransacked the place (9/11). They found the perpetrator and brought him to justice (Afghan War) . Then, got all noble and tried to make sure it never happens to anyone else's business (Iraq War). The Hoagie Hut closed for a while for repairs (less revenue), health care costs went up for the employees (Medicare D), and they installed a kick-ass security system on their house and business (Homeland Security).

All of that on the (U.S.) credit card.

## Easy Money Leads To Big Oops

Times got rough.  Not just for the Smiths but for everyone.  Then, just a few years later, Joe's bank comes to him and says "Great news!  You qualify for a million dollar mortgage at only 3%!  Want a bigger house?  Home prices only go up, there is no risk in this.  Act now before this opportunity goes away!!"  

Joe says “hell yes!” without thinking. His kids were growing and he wanted more space.  And, he's been renting forever so time to start owning and making some equity, right?  After all, the bank said there was no risk.  They would know.

So, Joe buys a sweet crib for a cool million.  The bank says “Congratulations on your new home!” -- then sells his mortgage to his uncle's retirement fund a week later.  Joe's uncle now owns part of his home.

Then, that 3% teaser rate skyrockets to 9%. Suddenly, the Smiths' mortgage triples and Joe tries to sell the home.  No one buys.  The scenario Joe never thought would happen -- happens.  His home is worth less than a million -- way less. 

Worse yet, the health inspector rates the Hoagie Hut a risk because he saw a rat.  The bad press loses even more customers (recession).

Joe's uncle, Sam, gets a statement from his retirement fund.  It declined 50% in value and probably won't go back up anytime soon.  Sam has done well in his career and has a good amount of savings but it still stings nonetheless.

Joe sells his million dollar home for $500K taking a massive loss.  Joe goes back to renting a home -- back to no equity and 3x more debt (from the original $122K).   Joe can't even make his interest payments let alone rent.

Because Joe can't pay, Sam's retirement fund is cut in half -- because the Joe's of the world are all defaulting.  Uncle Sam chips in and pays a big chunk of Joe's debt so he can make payments. (ie:  TARP).

Welcome to the Subprime Lending Crisis!

## How To Get There
This is the "Step by Step" part that you've been patiently waiting for.

Step 1..of 1:  Think about Joe's Hoagie Hut.

How does he double his output without increasing his debt?

Yes, his $106K spend may go up.  But, remember, that's not part of the ratio.  It's just "Debt" to "Gross".  So, his spend goes up but his debt stays the same.  That's fine, it doesn't change the ratio.  As long as Joe spends less than he makes, he's not borrowing.  No new debt.  Joe just has more gross income but didn't use a debt vehicle to get there.

And, he's not paying down any debt either.  He could, and that would help.  But, Joe would prefer to invest any additional money he has to grow more.
