## 1. What Is the EU AI Act?

The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) is the world's first comprehensive legal framework regulating artificial intelligence. It officially entered into force on August 1, 2024, and establishes binding, risk-based rules for the development, deployment, and use of AI systems across all 27 EU member states.

This is not a directive that requires each country to write its own version — it's a regulation, meaning it's directly applicable law across the entire European Union without further local legislation. Think of it as the GDPR of AI.

### Core Architecture: The Risk-Based Framework

The Act sorts every AI system into one of four risk tiers, and your obligations scale with the risk level:

**Unacceptable Risk (BANNED)**
Social scoring by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces (with narrow exceptions), subliminal manipulation, exploitation of vulnerable groups. No compliance path — these are prohibited outright. Effective February 2, 2025.

**High Risk**
AI in hiring/employment, credit scoring, law enforcement, migration management, education access, critical infrastructure safety components. Requires conformity assessments, risk management systems, human oversight, transparency, data governance, and mandatory registration in an EU database.

**Limited Risk**
Chatbots, deepfakes, emotion recognition systems, AI-generated content. Transparency obligations — users must be told they're interacting with AI or viewing AI-generated content.

**Minimal Risk**
Spam filters, AI in video games, inventory management. No specific obligations. Voluntary codes of conduct encouraged.

### General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

The Act includes a separate regime for foundation models and general-purpose AI (think GPT, Claude, Gemini). All GPAI providers must publish training data summaries, comply with EU copyright law, and provide technical documentation. Models classified as posing "systemic risk" (trained with compute > 10^25 FLOPs) face additional obligations including adversarial testing, incident reporting, and cybersecurity measures. These GPAI rules became applicable August 2, 2025.

### Implementation Timeline

- **Aug 1, 2024** — AI Act enters into force
- **Feb 2, 2025** — Prohibited AI practices banned; AI literacy obligations begin
- **Aug 2, 2025** — GPAI model rules apply; governance structure and penalty framework active
- **Aug 2, 2026** — Full applicability — all remaining provisions, including high-risk AI system rules
- **Aug 2, 2027** — Extended deadline for high-risk AI embedded in regulated products (medical devices, automotive, etc.)

## 2. Who's Championing It?

The AI Act wasn't the work of a single politician — it was a multi-institutional effort across the EU's three legislative bodies. But there are clear names to know.

### The Architects

**European Commission:** Proposed the original legislation in April 2021 as part of its broader digital strategy. Ursula von der Leyen's Commission made AI regulation a priority. Margrethe Vestager (Executive VP for "A Europe Fit for the Digital Age") and Thierry Breton (Commissioner for Internal Market) were the two Commission heavyweights driving it forward.

**European Parliament Co-Rapporteurs:** Brando Benifei (S&D, Italy) and Dragoș Tudorache (Renew Europe, Romania) were the co-rapporteurs — essentially the lead negotiators who shepherded the legislation through Parliament. These two are the closest thing to individual "champions" of the Act. They managed the committee work, the amendments, the floor debates, and the trilogue negotiations with the Council.

**Shadow Rapporteurs:** Eva Maydell, Marcel Kolaja, and Axel Voss also played significant roles in shaping the Parliament's position.

**Council of the EU:** The Spanish presidency (second half of 2023) played a critical role in brokering the final trilogue deal. Carme Artigas, Spain's Secretary of State for Digitalization and AI, was a key figure in getting the Council and Parliament to align.

### The Trilogue Process

Important context for US readers: EU legislation goes through a "trilogue" — a three-way negotiation between the Commission (proposes), the Parliament (amends), and the Council (represents member states). The AI Act went through marathon trilogue sessions in late 2023, with the political agreement reached on December 9, 2023. The final text was approved by Parliament on March 13, 2024, with 523 votes in favor, 46 against, and 49 abstentions.

## 3. How Does It Actually Get Enforced?

Enforcement is a dual-layer system: EU-level oversight plus national-level enforcement.

### EU-Level: The European AI Office

Established within the European Commission, the AI Office is the central enforcement body with real teeth. It can evaluate AI models, demand technical documentation from providers, require corrective measures for non-compliance, and recommend sanctions or fines. Its primary focus is on GPAI models with systemic risk — the big foundation models.

### National Level: Member State Authorities

Each member state must designate National Competent Authorities (NCAs) by August 2, 2025. These include a Notifying Authority (handles conformity assessment bodies) and a Market Surveillance Authority (oversees day-to-day compliance). This is the on-the-ground enforcement — think of it as the national-level police for AI compliance.

### Penalty Structure

The fine structure is tiered and designed to hurt:

- **Prohibited AI practices:** Up to €35 million or 7% worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher)
- **Other AI Act obligations:** Up to €15 million or 3% worldwide annual turnover
- **Misleading information to authorities:** Up to €7.5 million or 1% worldwide annual turnover

For SMEs and startups, the same percentages apply but whichever amount is LOWER (not higher). This is a deliberate carve-out to avoid crushing small companies.

Key point: This is extraterritorial. If you're a US company deploying AI that affects EU citizens or is used within the EU market, you're subject to this. Same playbook as GDPR.

## 4. EU vs US: Why Can the EU Do This?

### [SETUP NOTE] The Big Difference Between EU and US

The single most important thing to understand before anything else in this section: the EU's regulatory culture is *preventative* — they build the guardrails before the cars are on the road. The US is *reactive* — they wait for the crash, then figure out who to sue. This isn't a value judgment. It's a structural reality that flows from how each system is built, and it explains nearly every difference that follows. Keep this framing front and center when writing the post — it's the thesis of this entire section.

---

This is where it gets interesting from a structural standpoint. The EU and US have fundamentally different governance architectures that produce fundamentally different regulatory outcomes.

### The EU's Structural Advantage for Regulation

**Supranational Authority:** The EU functions as a supranational union where regulations adopted at the EU level are directly binding on all 27 member states. No individual country gets to opt out. The AI Act is a Regulation (not a Directive), meaning it doesn't require local transposition — it IS the law everywhere, immediately.

**Precautionary Principle:** The EU's regulatory philosophy is preventative. They regulate BEFORE harm occurs. The operating assumption is that trust and safety are prerequisites for adoption and long-term economic benefit. This is baked into the EU's DNA — it's the same principle behind GDPR, REACH (chemicals), and food safety regulations.

**Single Market Mandate:** The EU's internal market justification gives it enormous regulatory reach. Anything that could fragment the single market or create barriers to trade across member states is fair game for EU-level regulation.

**Rights-First Framework:** The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights provides constitutional backing. The AI Act explicitly ties its risk categories to fundamental rights protections — privacy, non-discrimination, human dignity.

### Why the US Can't (or Won't) Do the Same

**Federalism:** The US Constitution reserves most regulatory authority to states unless explicitly granted to the federal government. There is no clear constitutional hook for comprehensive federal AI regulation the way the EU has its single market and fundamental rights mandates.

**Innovation-First Philosophy:** The US regulatory tradition sees government's role as removing obstacles to innovation, not proactively shaping markets. Both Democratic and Republican administrations have favored light-touch, sector-specific approaches over comprehensive horizontal regulation.

**Industry Lobbying:** Big Tech has enormous political influence in Washington. The EU's regulatory bodies are more insulated from direct industry lobbying than US Congress, where tech companies are among the largest campaign donors.

**Litigation-Based Enforcement:** The US defaults to ex-post enforcement through lawsuits rather than ex-ante regulation. Without explicit legal authority to regulate AI algorithms, the US approach relies on novel litigation — which is slow, inconsistent, and often jurisdiction-specific.

### The Resulting Landscape

The EU has one comprehensive law. The US has a patchwork. In 2025 alone, 1,208 AI-related bills were introduced across all 50 US states, with 145 enacted into law. Major examples include Colorado's AI Act (first comprehensive state AI law, effective June 30, 2026), California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act (SB 53, effective January 1, 2026), and Illinois' HB 3773 (amending the Human Rights Act to cover AI discrimination in employment).

## 5. Current US Federal Proposals

While there's no US equivalent to the AI Act yet, momentum is building — though the approach is very different.

### The December 2025 Executive Order

On December 11, 2025, President Trump signed an executive order titled "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence." This is significant not because it creates regulation, but because it signals the federal government's intent to PREEMPT state AI laws. Key elements:

- Mobilizes the DOJ to identify and challenge "onerous" state AI laws
- Discourages state enactment and enforcement of AI laws that conflict with federal policy
- Directs a Secretary's report expected to flag comprehensive frameworks in California, Colorado, and Texas as problematic
- Advances federal preemption through litigation and agency action
- Presses Congress to enact a uniform national framework

Important nuance: This EO doesn't CREATE regulation. It's an anti-regulation move at the state level, clearing the deck for a federal framework that's expected to be much lighter-touch than anything the states have passed.

### The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act (March 2026)

Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN) released a discussion draft on March 18, 2026 — the most ambitious congressional attempt at comprehensive federal AI governance to date. Full name: "Republic Unifying Meritocratic Performance Advancing Machine Intelligence by Eliminating Regulatory Interstate Chaos Across American Industry Act."

#### The "4 Cs" Framework

The bill is organized around protecting Children, Creators, Conservatives, and Communities:

**Children:** Restricts platforms from conducting market/product research on children under 13. Requires parental consent for research on minors up to 17.

**Creators:** Declares that unauthorized use of copyrighted works for AI training does NOT qualify as fair use. Protects voice and visual likeness from unauthorized digital replicas.

**Conservatives:** Codifies Trump's EO preventing "woke AI" in federal procurement. Requires government-procured LLMs to be "truthful" and "neutral."

**Communities:** Requires quarterly reports on AI-related job displacement to the Department of Labor. Directs the Secretary of Energy to manage data center impacts on consumer energy rates.

#### Other Key Provisions

**Duty of Care:** Mandates AI developers exercise "reasonable care" in design, development, and operation to prevent "foreseeable harm" to users.

**Risk Assessments:** Requires regular assessment of advanced AI risks with safety protocol reporting to the Department of Homeland Security.

**Federal Preemption:** Would preempt certain state AI laws — this is the big structural play. Consolidates regulatory authority at the federal level.

Status: This is a discussion draft, not yet introduced as formal legislation. Expected timeline for any federal AI law: late 2026 to early 2027 at the earliest.

## 6. Sentiment Tie-In: Where This Connects

This is where the AI Act thread feeds directly into the broader sentiment work. A few angles to develop:

### Public Sentiment Angles

**Trust Gap:** The EU's precautionary approach reflects a fundamentally different public sentiment toward technology. European publics tend to be more skeptical of unregulated tech, more privacy-conscious, and more supportive of government intervention. US sentiment is more polarized — split between techno-optimism and growing unease about job displacement, deepfakes, and surveillance.

**The "Protection vs Innovation" Narrative:** Sentiment on AI regulation maps almost perfectly onto existing political fault lines. In the US, the framing is often "regulation kills innovation" vs "unregulated AI threatens democracy." The EU has largely resolved this tension in favor of regulation-as-enabler. This framing difference is a rich vein for sentiment analysis.

**Copyright as a Proxy War:** Both the EU Act and the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act address copyright, but from different philosophical bases. Public sentiment around AI training on copyrighted works is overwhelmingly negative among creators and mixed among consumers. This is one of the few areas where there's transatlantic sentiment alignment.

**Employment Anxiety:** AI job displacement fears are consistently among the top public concerns globally. Both regulatory frameworks acknowledge this — the EU through risk classification of employment AI, the US through reporting requirements. Sentiment data on this will be critical for the reports and posts.

### Report & Post Connections

- The AI Act's risk framework can serve as an analytical lens for categorizing AI sentiment by use case
- The EU vs US regulatory divergence mirrors and reinforces existing sentiment divergence in polling data
- The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act's "4 Cs" framework reveals which sentiment vectors US politicians believe are most electorally salient
- The state-level patchwork in the US creates differential sentiment environments that are measurable and mappable
- Enforcement timelines create natural inflection points for tracking sentiment shifts (Feb 2025, Aug 2025, Aug 2026)

## Appendix A: China's Social Credit System — Why It Matters Here

### The Direct Connection to the EU AI Act

This isn't a tangent. China's social credit system is literally the thing the EU AI Act was designed to prevent. When the Act bans "social scoring by public authorities" as an unacceptable risk — the very first category in the prohibition tier — they're pointing directly at what China has built. It's the cautionary example that animates the entire regulatory framework.

### What It Actually Is

The most important misconception to clear up first: it's not a single unified "score" like a credit score. It's a network of interconnected systems — blacklists, redlists (whitelists), and administrative enforcement tools — run across multiple government agencies at the national and local level.

The core infrastructure is the National Credit Information Sharing Platform (NCISP), managed by the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC). By early 2025, it had collected over 80.7 billion records covering approximately 180 million businesses. The system runs on an integrated data network connecting government agencies, regulators, courts, and businesses.

### How It Works in Practice

There are three main types of blacklists: the judgment defaulter blacklist (people who don't comply with court orders), sectoral blacklists (industry-specific violations), and no-fly/no-ride lists.

**What happens when you're on the blacklist:**

- Banned from air travel and high-speed rail. As of June 2019, 26.82 million air tickets and 5.96 million high-speed rail tickets had been denied to "untrustworthy" individuals.
- Blocked from purchasing property, vacation packages, or private school tuition for your children.
- Banned from high-end hotels and luxury goods on e-commerce platforms.
- Barred from government subsidies, honorary titles, civil service positions, and upper management roles at state-owned enterprises.
- Prohibited from applying for credit cards.
- Public humiliation — mugshots displayed on LED screens on buildings or shown before movies in theaters. Personal information deliberately made publicly accessible online and at public venues.

### Real Cases

**Lawyer Li Xiaolin (2016):** Tried to buy a plane ticket using his national ID card. System rejected it — he'd been blacklisted for failing to carry out a court order in 2015. Stranded 1,200 miles from home. After writing a second apology letter, he was removed from the travel blacklist but remained on another list blocking credit card applications.

**Journalist Liu Hu (2015):** Sued for reposting a social media message deemed defamatory. After wiring compensation money to the wrong account, the court blacklisted him. Liu Hu had been reporting on government corruption. Consequences: banned from buying property and from air/rail travel.

**Jia Yueting (business executive):** After failing to comply with a court order regarding his debt-ridden company's obligations, he was banned from purchasing "luxury" goods and all air/high-speed rail travel for a year.

### 2025-2026 Developments

The system is evolving. On March 31, 2025, the Communist Party's Central Committee and State Council released guidelines aimed at unifying the disparate pilot projects, strengthening legal foundations, and (notably) striking a balance between governance and privacy protection. The NDRC also issued Credit Repair Management Measures effective April 1, 2026, with formal rules on classification, publication periods, and a workflow for repair applications.

The current policy focus in 2026 is more heavily weighted toward the *corporate* social credit system than the personal one — tracking business trustworthiness, regulatory compliance, and financial obligations. The personal side exists but is less uniformly implemented than Western media tends to portray.

### Why This Matters for the Thread

This is the extreme end of the spectrum. When we talk about AI regulation and public sentiment, China's system is the thing people picture when they fear government overreach with AI. The EU explicitly legislated against it. The US hasn't even gotten close to building something like it, but the *fear* of it drives a huge chunk of anti-regulation sentiment ("if we give government any authority over AI, we end up like China") AND pro-regulation sentiment ("if we don't regulate, private companies will build their own version"). Both sides use China as the boogeyman. That makes it essential context for the sentiment analysis.
