## What is SSDI
In short, it is when a worker claims a disability --however they got it -- that prevents them for going back to work in a meaningful capacity.  At this point, the Federal government provides a subsidy for that former-worker to live, along with health care -- eventually.  There's a 5-month waiting period before your first SSDI check arrives.  And Medicare?  That's a 24-month wait *after* your SSDI kicks in.  So you're looking at roughly 30 months from the day you become disabled before you have government health coverage.  In the meantime, you're on your own -- Medicaid if you qualify, Marketplace if you can afford it.  Oh, and if you're wondering what happens during that gap: more than 1 in 10 SSDI recipients die within their first year on the program.  The cliff is real.

Now, that person is living off the government.  And, putting it bluntly, you.  Your tax dollars covers this federal benefit as a part of the [Social Security Amendments of 1956](/data/fact-check/), signed by Eisenhower.  Originally, you had to be over 50 and permanently disabled.  By 1960, they dropped the age requirement entirely.  The program has been expanding ever since.  You can now "retire".

But, here in lies the problem.  The country needs growth.  Growth needs workers. Workers add to the GDP which, in turn, decreases the Debt to GDP ratio. ([I wrote about that here](/blog/national-debt/)).  There's a large pool here of workers that can no longer do one job --  but they are not worthless.  Call me naive but I generally think that most people *want* to make money.  Not only to raise their standard of living but also provide them a reason to get up in the morning.

But I get, and empathetic to, disablements.  By no means would I ever force a disabled worker to perform something they physically or mentally shouldn't do.  That's just wrong.  So, I won't be going into any of that here.  You will not here me say "put them all back to work!" -- nope.  

Gotta better idea.

## How It Works Today
Let's start with some facts:

- **8.1 million** people are on SSDI today
- **~2,000 per day** will enter the program (roughly 595,000 new awards per year)
- **Less than 1%** will ever return to work.  About 20 people per day.
- **84-91%** of all exits are death or aging into Social Security retirement
- The average monthly benefit is **$1,580**.  Max is about $4,150.
- The program has been around since **1956** -- nearly 70 years -- and has paid out to over **18.8 million** people in the last 25 years alone

Interesting that the vast majority of people enter SSDI will simply just enter Social Security when they qualify, extending their benefits for life.  Or, die.  At least, with the latter, they no longer have to be covered.  No more cost to you.  But, for those that enter full retirement, good on them.  That's not where we're headed in this post.

However, there is a substantial population that doesn't qualify for retirements (less than 62, at least) that are not working due to the disability.  So, my next rabbit hole AI question was:

It's a mixed bag but there are large clusters of people in muskioskelular and other physical ailments.  Again, I get it.  They don't need to be framing new homes.

But, what if there was job that worked -- for that individual's needs?

And, what if that job paid 2 or 3 times what they are getting now, with health benefits?

And, what if that job was in the private industry -- no government involvment.

Stating the obvious, the worker would gain a substantial increase in income, covered under a company's health plan, and relieves the government burden.  Sounds pretty good, eh?

Let's say the 2.76 million people with musculoskeletal disabilities -- the largest group on SSDI by far -- and RTW half of them in the scenario I described.  That's 1.38 million workers back in the economy.  What would the outputs be?

By raising that 1% to 17%, the government reduces $26.2 billion of actual spend per year.  Putting into more context, if you make $100K a year, you will save roughly $157 on your tax bill.  Not life changing for you -- but $26B back into the economy is a different story.

So, why isn't this happening now?


MY NOTES:

**Key Numbers:**

- Beneficiary trend: 6M → 10.2M peak → 8.6M now (decline from death/aging, NOT return to work)
- Monthly intake: ~49,500 (peaked 85,500/mo in 2010)
- RTW rate: never exceeded 1% of total pool in any state, any year, 22 years of data
- Died + Aged Out = 84–91% of all terminations, consistently
- 18.8 million people awarded SSDI over 25 years
- State RTW range: 0.2% (West Virginia) to 1.5% (DC)

**The Hook:** "2,000 people will enter the disability system tomorrow. Only 200 of them will ever go back to work."

**Policy Proposal — Private Sector Tax Credit:**

- Company hires SSDI beneficiary, gets $15K Year 1 tax credit
- Must employ minimum 6 months
- Government saves ~$18K/yr per person off rolls + gains ~$6.5K tax revenue = $25K swing per person
- $15K credit pays for itself in 7 months even at 1-in-3 success rate
- Employers naturally cherry-pick most employable first (back injuries who can WFH, managed mental health, recession casualties)
- Pool self-sorts to truly disabled in 3–5 years without any bureaucrat making subjective calls

**State Pilot Angle:**

- Washington: tech heavy, "$70K to sit at a desk and tag training data"
- West Virginia: turn the state with the most disability into the state best at managing healthcare data via AI

**The Two Charts Concept:** Rich intake data (25 years of claims, awards, demographics) vs. a blank page for outcomes — nobody is tracking return to work, and that's the problem.
