Why would anyone aim for mediocrity? Isn't that... giving up? Nope. It's actually the most practical advice I can give you, and it's how I've approached learning for decades. Let me explain.

The picture above is my first DIY project as a home owner. I had never built a walkway, only read about it. So, I started by planning, procuring, digging, and laying bricks. The end result was hardly level, wobbly, and the bricks didn't match the house. Mediocre, but I was proud of it. 3 different owners and 18 years later, that walkway still stands.

## Everyone Starts at Zero

Think about it: there was a time when Tom Brady had never thrown a football, Picasso had never picked up a brush, and Taylor Swift didn't know she wanted to be a singer. Every "great" started with nothing.

I imagine Brady's first pass was to his dad, maybe five feet away, barely a spiral. Over time, five feet became ten, then twenty. High school brought distance. College brought precision. The NFL brought the ability to read defenses while five 300-pound men charged at him.

So when was he "great"? When was he even "good"? The answer: it took years of dedication to one thing. And here's my honest reaction to that: That's a lot of friggin' work.

##   

## I Don't Want to Be Tom Brady

No disrespect to the GOAT, but I don't want to be Tom Brady at anything. To become the best at something, you sacrifice everything else. I'd rather have variety. I'd rather be competent at many things than elite at one.

Even for my professional career, striving to be the best Data Engineer in the world is is futile. Tech changes so rapidly that, in order to be the best, I would have to keep up with it *and* be able to apply it. The reality is that companies don't like constant change just for the sake of it. They need to invest in a particular technology for a while to reap the benefits before migrating to a new stack.

This isn't settling—it's a choice. I'm a habitual hobbyist. I play guitar, bass, keyboards, synth, and yes, even a Theremin. I grow microgreens and cultivate mushrooms in my greenhouse. I fish, or try to. I've started tinkering with AI modeling but I'll never know it as well as the top engineers at Google. None of these make me world-class at anything, and I'm genuinely okay with that.

## The Ladder Everyone Climbs

Here's the framework I keep in my head:

Start → Mediocre → Good → Great → Best

Every single expert climbed this ladder. No exceptions. Brady was mediocre once. So was every teammate who helped him win those seven rings—and notice how none of them are called the GOAT of their position. They were good enough to contribute to something bigger.

Society tells you to be the best, but what people actually need is for you to be good enough to be part of a team. One "best," surrounded by a bunch of "good enoughs."

## Why Mediocrity Matters

Here's the insight that changed how I approach projects:

Mediocrity gets me past "Start."

That's it. That's the whole philosophy.

I don't know if I'll ever get to "Good" on any given project. I definitely don't think about "Great" or "Best." I just aim for mediocre, and then I reassess. Maybe I'm fine staying there forever. Maybe I quit. Maybe I push further. But because I started, I now have a choice.

Before starting, there's no choice. There's just... nothing.

## You Define Mediocre

Here's the part most people miss: mediocre isn't a universal standard. It's yours to set.

My "mediocre" at building isn't yours. Your "mediocre" at golf isn't mine. The bar moves based on what you need to feel like you've actually done the thing.

Which means the only way to truly fail is to never start. Everything else is just calibration.

Here's what that looks like for me:

Golf: Didn't play until after college. Was terrible. Practiced just enough to not embarrass myself or slow down the group. I count balls lost, not strokes. Best round ever was 89—the only time I ever broke 100. I'll play if you're paying, but don't count on me if you need "good" partners.

Bass Guitar: Picked it up a few years ago. My guitar skills translate a little, but the more I learn, the more I realize I've barely started. I can play simple songs. By my definition, I'm not even mediocre yet—and that's fine.

Building: People see my Office Shed and Greenhouse and argue with me on this one. But it's my definition, not theirs. I know they're semi-good at best. But I started, I finished, and they work. The next build may actually reach "Good." Again, based on my definition.

Three different spots on the ladder. One thing in common: I decided what "enough" looked like. So can you.

## Starting Reveals What Doesn't Stick

Starting many things isn't scattered energy—it's a filtering system. You learn what doesn't give you value, which clarifies what does.

I've started plenty of things. Skydiving? Got mediocre. Stopped. No regret. Snowboarding? Same story. Golf? Another one. All on the shelf now.

But contrast that with what survived the filter: bike touring, fishing, gardening, mushrooms. These are the ones I keep coming back to. These are the ones that give me something.

The insight: **Mediocrity isn't just a stepping stone to greatness. Sometimes it's the answer.**

By starting and aiming for mediocre, I discover what actually sticks. I don't waste years chasing something that doesn't fulfill me. I give it a fair shot, get competent enough to know, and then decide. That's way more valuable than defaulting to nothing.

## The Ladder Guides Your Wallet

So what does all this mean practically? It guides how I spend.

Every time I go into Guitar Center, trouble is brewing. So many beautiful toys hanging from the walls... I need one. Then I look at the price tag. Nope, don't need that. Because I'm only mediocre/good status.

For guitar, I already have a substantial collection and no new guitar will instantly make me better. For the record, I have 10 guitars (8 guitars, 2 bass) and spent less than $1500 for all of them. That's $150/guitar—market value on Facebook. Even though in certain scenarios I'm "not bad," a $1000 guitar won't make me any better. Practice will.

So the next time you covet that $700 Fender Strat, look at the Squires on marketplaces for $200 tops. If you're just starting out, you're not even worth that.

You can equate your investment to the level you're at. If you haven't started, invest the bare minimum (<$100). If you're progressing toward mediocre, raise the investment accordingly.

Remember, you're probably not "good" yet—so that $700 guitar could be something you want but aren't good enough for. Or do what I do: get another $150 guitar that's different. Every guitar I have is fundamentally different (nylon, steel, electric) and gives me options across genres. That's where I get value—not from a true $5,000 Gibson '59 Les Paul but my $400 Epiphone Les Paul --the most money I've spent on any one guitar-- kicks some serious ass.

Same principle elsewhere. Golf? "I'll play if you pay." I don't need more investment—I'm happy with my status. Building? I hit the culled wood section for 70% discounts before anything else.

The ladder guides what you should invest. Don't start at "Good"—because you're not.

## What This Series Is About

This is my space to share experiences of starting things—projects, hobbies, skills, ideas. Some will stay mediocre. Some might get good. A few might surprise me.

The goal isn't to inspire you to greatness. It's to remind you (and myself) that starting is the hardest part, and "good enough" might be all you need.

So go ahead. Aim for mediocre. You can always climb higher later—but only if you're already on the ladder.
