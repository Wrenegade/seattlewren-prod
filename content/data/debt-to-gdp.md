## What You're Looking At

The debt-to-GDP ratio — not the raw dollar amount of debt — is how economists measure whether a country's debt is sustainable.  A country can owe more dollars every single year and still be in better shape, as long as the economy grows faster than the debt.

That's exactly what happened between 1946 and 2001.  The total debt kept climbing, but GDP grew faster.  The ratio dropped from 119% after WWII to 55% by 2001 — without anyone "paying it off."

Then it reversed.

The shaded regions highlight the major economic eras: the post-war boom and women entering the workforce (driving the ratio down), the stagnation and financial crises (driving it back up), and the pandemic surge.

**The key takeaway:**  The ratio drops when the economy grows.  The economy grows when more people work.  That's the lever.
